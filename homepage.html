Historical Timeline

The evolution of mathematical thinking across different eras

1500 BCE - 500 BCE

Vedic Period

The origins of Indian mathematics can be traced to the Vedic period, where mathematical concepts were developed for constructing fire altars and performing rituals. The Sulba Sutras contained early geometric principles and approximations of irrational numbers.

500 BCE - 500 CE

Classical Period

This era saw significant advancements with works like the Bakshali Manuscript. Mathematicians began developing sophisticated number systems, algebra, and trigonometry. The concept of zero as a number and place value system emerged during this time.

500 CE - 1200 CE

Golden Age of Indian Mathematics

The most prolific period for Indian mathematics, featuring luminaries like Aryabhata, Brahmagupta, and Bhaskara II. Concepts of calculus, advanced algebra, and astronomical mathematics were developed. The decimal system was fully established and spread to the Arab world and Europe.

1200 CE - 1800 CE

Medieval Period

The Kerala School of Astronomy and Mathematics flourished during this time, making groundbreaking contributions to infinite series, calculus, and trigonometry. Mathematicians like Madhava of Sangamagrama developed series expansions for trigonometric functions centuries before European mathematicians.

1800 CE - Present

Modern Era

In the modern era, Indian mathematicians have made significant contributions to global mathematics. Figures like Srinivasa Ramanujan revolutionized number theory, while others have excelled in fields ranging from algebraic geometry to computational mathematics.

Notable Mathematicians

Brilliant minds who changed the course of Indian mathematical history

Aryabhata

476-550 CE

First major mathematician-astronomer from the classical age of Indian mathematics. He wrote the Aryabhatiya, which covers arithmetic, algebra, and trigonometry. He calculated π to four decimal places and developed a place value system.

Learn more

Brahmagupta

598-668 CE

Pioneered the use of zero as a number, gave rules for computing with zero, and wrote rules for computing with negative numbers. His work Brahmasphutasiddhanta contains remarkable contributions to mathematics.

Learn more

Bhaskara II

1114-1185 CE

Known for his work in calculus, he was the first to conceptualize the derivative representing instantaneous change. His book Lilavati contains numerous problems on arithmetic, algebra, and geometry.

Learn more

Madhava of Sangamagrama

c. 1350-1425 CE

Founder of the Kerala School, he discovered infinite series expansions for trigonometric functions 300 years before Newton and Leibniz. He calculated π to 11 decimal places.

Learn more

Srinivasa Ramanujan

1887-1920

Self-taught mathematical genius who made extraordinary contributions to mathematical analysis, number theory, infinite series, and continued fractions. He independently compiled nearly 3,900 results during his short lifetime.

Learn more

C.R. Rao

1920-Present

Pioneering figure in statistics, known for theory of estimation, Cramer-Rao inequality, and contributions to statistical genetics and biometry. His work has influenced fields from economics to medical research.

— Srinivasa Ramanujan

— Bhaskara II

"Just as the sun eclipses the stars by its brilliance, so the man of knowledge will eclipse the fame of others in assemblies of the people if he proposes algebraic problems, and still more if he solves them."
